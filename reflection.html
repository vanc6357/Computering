Reflection
As a particle, light can:
-Reflect
-Refract (bend)

Reflection:

Flat mirror reflection



Law of reflection:
θi = θr

Steps to draw a flat mirror reflection:

1. Draw the reflected image
2. Draw a reflected ray from the image to the eye of observer
3. Draw a normal line where the ray intersects the mirror
4. Measure the incident angle between the ray and the normal line
5. Draw the reflected ray on the other side of the normal line using θi = θr
6. The incident ray should intersect with the object

Field of vision of a flat mirror



The colored area is the field of vision provided by the mirror

Note: The field of vision is always between incident angles
To find how many images are reflected by two mirrors facing each other at an angle, use the formula:

n = 360/θ- 1
Reflection in curved mirrors:




Concave mirrors are converging. Convex mirrors are diverging.

F = focal length
C = center of curvature (2F)
Principal axis = line that passes through the center of the mirror at a 90o angle.

Concave mirror reflection:




distance from object to mirror (do) > 0

distance from image to mirror (di) > 0

height of object (ho) > 0

height of the image (hi) < 0

magnification (M) < 0


Magnification formula:


Note: With lenses and mirrors, di is negative when the image is virtual.
Di is negative when an object is reflected on the inside of a mirror, or on the outside of a lens.
Concave field of vision:



Since the center of curvature is closer to the eye than the mirror, the field of vision is converging.
Convex mirror reflection:



do > 0

di < 0

ho > 0

hi > 0

M > 0

Since the focal point is on the inside of the mirror, the rays are still aligned towards the focal point, but reflected outwards.
In this case, the image is virtual since the rays converge inside the mirror.

Convex field of vision:



Since the center of curvature is further away from the eye than the mirror, the field of vision is diverging.
