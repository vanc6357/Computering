Refraction
Refraction: The bending of light






Snell's law

n1V1 = n2V2

n = index of refraction
V = speed of light

To find n:

n = c/V

c = speed of light in vacuum = 300,000,000 m/s

Refraction from one medium to another:



n1 Sinθ = n2 Sinθ

The larger the angle of the refracted ray, the faster the light is travelling.

Critical angle:
The biggest incident angle before total internal reflection occurs.

Total internal reflection happens when the incident ray goes from a dense medium to a less dense medium, and is reflected off the less dense medium rather than refracted though it.



In the three diagrams above, a ray is depicted going through a dense medium to another less dense medium. It is confirmed that the second medium in less dense since the ray is refracted away from the normal line.

In the first picture, a dim reflected ray is depicted as well as a refracted ray. This is because some light is always reflected as well as refracted. As the incident angle increases, the reflected ray gets brighter and brighter, and the refracted ray gets dimmer and dimmer. When the incident angle reaches a certain size (critical angle), the refracted ray will end up bending at a 90 o angle from the normal line. Thus, the refracted ray will disappear, and all the light will end up in the reflected ray. At this moment, total internal reflection will have occured.

Sinθc = Critical angle

Sinθc = n2/n1

Refraction in lenses:

Note: Lens are not like mirrors, as they do not reflect light. They refract it. If the image is reflected off a lens, then di is negative and the image is virtual.






Unlike in mirrors, biconcave lenses are diverging, and biconvex lenses are converging.

Thick lens refraction:




The normal line on a thick lens is tangential to the curve of the lens. Thus, the axis of the normal line will vary around a thick lens.

Thin lens approximation:
The thickness is negligible.



The normal line on a thin lens is perpendicular to the lens.
